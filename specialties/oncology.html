The patient and caregiver reality

Cancer decisions are researched for weeks before the first call.

A diagnosis, an abnormal scan, a worrying biopsy result. In most cases, the family searches extensively before contacting a hospital — and in 70%+ of enquiries, it's a caregiver doing the searching. Centres visible at each research stage with credible, MDT-quality content earn the enquiry.

🔍

Caregivers search "second opinion cancer" before they call

40–60% of cancer patients seek a second opinion before committing to treatment. Many caregivers search specifically for "second opinion oncologist Bangalore" or "best cancer hospital India" within days of diagnosis. Centres without visible MDT structure and published tumour board credentials are invisible at this high-intent moment.

💬

Cancer WhatsApp groups drive hospital referrals

Patient and caregiver cancer communities on WhatsApp, Facebook, and Reddit actively share hospital experiences and specialist recommendations. Word-of-mouth in oncology is amplified by digital communities — positive outcomes posted in these groups drive enquiries more reliably than paid media alone.

📋

Insurance navigation is a decision stage, not an afterthought

Oncology admissions frequently stall at insurance pre-authorization. Families researching cancer centres actively look for cashless network lists, TPA-approved hospitals, and cost transparency content. Centres that surface this information clearly reduce drop-off at the financial decision stage — often the final hurdle before booking.

Why generic oncology marketing doesn't convert

A "world-class oncology team" page doesn't win a second-opinion seeker.

Second-opinion economy

40–60% of cancer patients seek a second opinion before committing to treatment. Centres without visible MDT structure, tumour board credentials, and published outcomes lose these patients to Tata Memorial, Apollo, or Manipal before the consult is even booked. A "best oncology department" page isn't enough — subspecialty depth and documented multidisciplinary process is what earns the second-opinion call.

Insurance drag & cashless friction

Oncology admissions frequently stall at insurance pre-authorization. Centres that surface cashless network membership, MDT-approved treatment protocols, and clear cost transparency in their digital content reduce drop-off at the financial decision stage. Patients and caregivers who can't find insurance clarity often choose centres where it is clearly spelled out — regardless of clinical quality.

Caregiver-as-searcher reality

In 70%+ of cancer enquiries, the primary researcher is a family member — spouse, adult child, or sibling — not the patient. Content that only addresses the clinical pathway misses the caregiver's questions: prognosis conversations, logistics (how far, how long), financial planning, and emotional support resources. Caregiver-targeted Meta campaigns and dedicated WhatsApp nurture sequences address this reality directly.

Frequently asked questions · Oncology

What oncology heads and CMOs ask before engaging us.

How do you handle patient consent and privacy in oncology marketing content?
Every patient story we produce requires written informed consent obtained before the shoot, with patients reviewing and approving final edits before publication. We follow NMC guidelines on testimonials and avoid any content that implies guaranteed outcomes. Patient videos are produced with revocable consent and removed on request. Caregiver-as-searcher content is carefully framed to be informational, not exploitative — our oncology editorial guidelines are available for review before engagement.
What is the typical CPQL for oncology marketing in India?
Oncology CPQL typically ranges from ₹600 to ₹1,100 per qualified enquiry depending on cancer type, geography, and decision-journey stage. High-intent searches like 'second opinion cancer Bangalore' carry CPQLs at the lower end. Awareness-stage campaigns targeting caregivers run higher CPQL but build a larger long-term funnel. We report CPQL per cancer type and per channel monthly so you see where each rupee is most productive.
How do you help us compete with Tata Memorial, Apollo, and Manipal in oncology?
We build digital assets that signal what patients actually evaluate when comparing cancer centres: MDT structure, tumour board credentials, published case volumes, subspecialty depth, and patient-accessible outcomes summaries. We do not make claims that cannot be substantiated — instead, we help your programme surface verifiable differentiators that build credibility against national benchmarks. Positioning is earned through content depth, not brand spending.
How do you address the caregiver-as-searcher dynamic in oncology content?
In 70%+ of cancer enquiries the primary researcher is a family member — often a spouse, adult child, or sibling. Our oncology content strategy builds two tracks: a clinical track for the patient (treatment protocols, specialist credentials, side-effect management) and a caregiver track (prognosis conversations, logistics, financial planning, emotional support resources). Meta campaigns are audience-targeted toward caregiver demographics. WhatsApp nurture sequences address the family, not just the patient.
Can you help us build a GP and physician referral programme alongside digital marketing?
Yes. Physician referral is the dominant acquisition channel for oncology in India, and digital marketing should complement it. We build LinkedIn-based GP outreach programmes, MDT update communications, tumour board case summary newsletters, and referring-physician WhatsApp groups — measured through the same CPQL framework so you see how each channel contributes to total enquiry volume.
How do you handle insurance and cashless content ethically in oncology?
We create informational content — cashless network membership lists, pre-authorization guidance, TPA contact flows, and a plain-language explainer on what insurance typically covers in oncology — that reduces the financial anxiety causing patients to delay or disengage. This content is factual and regularly updated. We do not make guarantees on coverage and clearly flag that individual policy terms vary. Transparency builds trust; ambiguity loses patients to better-positioned competitors.
