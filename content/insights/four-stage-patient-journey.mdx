Most hospital marketing decks have a funnel diagram on slide three. Awareness on top, conversion at the bottom, a long arrow joining the two. The diagram is wrong — not in its shapes, but in the way it pretends a patient&rsquo;s decision is a single, linear collapse from curiosity to booking. It isn&rsquo;t.

A patient who searches *&ldquo;chest pain when to worry&rdquo;* at 11 p.m. is not the same person who, four days later, types *&ldquo;best cardiologist Indiranagar reviews.&rdquo;* And neither of them is the person who, after the consult, lies awake wondering whether the angiogram their doctor mentioned is really necessary.

Three different decisions. Three different assets. Three different metrics. A funnel asks marketing to treat them as one. The four-stage map insists on treating them apart.

## The four stages, briefly

We work to a four-stage map: **Awareness, Consideration, Decision, Post-consult.** Each stage has its own search behaviour, its own emotional register, and its own asset taxonomy. Treating them separately is not a creative flourish; it&rsquo;s the only way to keep a campaign honest about what it&rsquo;s doing.

### 01 — Awareness: *&ldquo;Something feels off.&rdquo;*

The patient is not yet thinking about your hospital. They&rsquo;re thinking about themselves. Their queries are symptom-shaped — *&ldquo;left arm tingling,&rdquo;* *&ldquo;sudden weight loss,&rdquo;* *&ldquo;why does my heel hurt in the morning.&rdquo;* The right asset here is education: clear, calm, clinically reviewed. A symptom explainer. A short YouTube video. An article that ends with *&ldquo;here is when to see a doctor,&rdquo;* not *&ldquo;book at our hospital.&rdquo;*

The metric that matters is reach into the symptom-search vocabulary, not bookings. A hospital that wins this stage is the one whose name a patient remembers two weeks later when their GP says *&ldquo;you should get this looked at.&rdquo;*

Hospitals that pitch in this stage lose the patient permanently. The patient remembers the pitch, not the information.

### 02 — Consideration: *&ldquo;Which hospital, which doctor?&rdquo;*

Now the patient is comparing. They&rsquo;ve accepted they need care. Their queries are evaluative — credentials, distance, reviews, cost. The right asset here is trust: doctor profiles that read like profiles and not like CVs, patient stories with consent, procedure walkthroughs that don&rsquo;t flinch from cost.

The metric that matters is video view-through to enquiry — does the patient who watches your cardiologist explain a procedure pick up the phone? Hospitals lose patients here by being opaque. *&ldquo;Speak to our team for pricing&rdquo;* in 2026 is a bounce, not a lead.

The honest hospital wins by being more useful than the brochure. Show the procedure. Name the cost range. Introduce the surgeon. The patient who books after that is a patient who will refer.

### 03 — Decision: *&ldquo;I am ready to book.&rdquo;*

High-intent search, ad clicks, calls. The patient has chosen. The asset is friction-free intake — a landing page that matches the query exactly, a click-to-call button that works on mobile, a WhatsApp option for the patient who doesn&rsquo;t want to call.

The metric that matters is call-to-appointment conversion, tracked at specialty level. Hospitals lose patients here by routing high-intent traffic to a homepage. The homepage is for people who don&rsquo;t know what they want. The patient at the decision stage knows exactly what they want — they want to book a cardiology consult on a date they can attend, with a doctor whose name they have already typed.

This is also where the call centre matters more than the website. A 30-second wait before pickup is the difference between an appointment and a competitor&rsquo;s appointment.

### 04 — Post-consult: *&ldquo;What now?&rdquo;*

The consult is done. The patient has been told they need a follow-up, a test, a procedure. There is now a gap — sometimes hours, sometimes weeks — between knowing and doing. The asset is nurture: a WhatsApp sequence, an email reminder, a recovery playbook. A clinical-team check-in 48 hours after the consult. A specialty-tagged six-month re-engagement.

The metric that matters is twelve-month retention — does the patient come back for the test, the procedure, the annual check-in? Most hospitals do nothing here. This is the cheapest gap to close and the one most often left open.

A patient acquired and not retained costs five to seven times more per lifetime than a patient acquired and retained (Bain &amp; HubSpot retention benchmarks).

## What this map changes

The map is not a poster for the conference room. It changes what we build, and what we don&rsquo;t.

- A piece of content that doesn&rsquo;t map to a stage doesn&rsquo;t get made.
- A KPI that doesn&rsquo;t correspond to a stage&rsquo;s actual decision moment is a vanity metric.
- A campaign that targets the same audience across all four stages is, by definition, wasting most of its spend.

In practice: when we audit a hospital&rsquo;s YouTube channel, we sort every video by the stage it serves. The pile labelled *&ldquo;Awareness&rdquo;* is usually three videos and 600 hours of footage of doctors thanking award juries. That&rsquo;s the audit, in one sentence.

Hospitals that adopt the four-stage map get three things back inside a quarter: a content backlog organised by purpose, a KPI dashboard that explains why things are growing or shrinking, and an honest answer to the question *&ldquo;why isn&rsquo;t our marketing working?&rdquo;*

Usually the answer is *&ldquo;because three of the four stages are unstaffed.&rdquo;*

## What we measure at each stage

| Stage | Primary metric | Secondary metric |
|---|---|---|
| Awareness | Symptom-search reach | Branded recall lift |
| Consideration | Video view-through to enquiry | Doctor profile dwell time |
| Decision | Call-to-appointment rate | Specialty cost per qualified call |
| Post-consult | 12-month retention | Second-visit conversion |

A hospital running marketing without numbers in three of those four rows is a hospital that is going to keep wondering why its OPD numbers don&rsquo;t move.

## FAQ

**What is the four-stage patient journey?**
Awareness, Consideration, Decision, and Post-consult — the four distinct moments every patient moves through. Each has its own search behaviour, emotional register, asset type, and primary metric. Treating them as one collapses marketing into a generic funnel that under-converts every stage.

**Why is a funnel diagram wrong for healthcare marketing?**
A funnel implies a linear collapse from curiosity to booking. Healthcare decisions don&rsquo;t work that way. A patient at the awareness stage cannot be moved to the decision stage by a stronger ad — different intents need different assets. The four-stage map respects that the patient is, at each stage, a different person with a different question.

**Which stage do most Indian hospitals neglect?**
Post-consult. Most hospitals invest in awareness and decision stages and treat the consult as the finish line. The data says otherwise — patients lost between consult and follow-up cost five to seven times more to re-acquire than to retain.

**How do you measure success at each stage?**
Symptom-search reach (Awareness), video view-through to enquiry (Consideration), call-to-appointment rate (Decision), and twelve-month retention (Post-consult). Anything more granular is engineering. Anything coarser is a vanity metric.

**Can the four-stage map be applied to a single specialty, or only to the whole hospital?**
Specialty-first is more useful. Cardiology has a different patient journey than fertility, which has a different patient journey than orthopaedics. Run the map per specialty, then aggregate.
