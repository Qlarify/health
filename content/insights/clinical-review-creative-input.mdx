The most expensive moment in hospital content production is the one most agencies engineer last: the clinical review.

You&rsquo;ve commissioned a writer. They&rsquo;ve produced 1,800 words on, say, the early signs of stroke. The piece is in editorial-house style — sharp, accessible, well-structured. It goes to the consultant for clinical sign-off. The consultant reads it carefully, then sends back a list of nineteen corrections, four of which are factually load-bearing and the other fifteen of which would change the structure of the article entirely.

The writer is now defensive. The consultant is now annoyed. The article is now three weeks late. Eventually it ships, in a form none of the original participants are happy with — and the lesson the hospital learns is *&ldquo;clinical review is slow.&rdquo;* The lesson it should learn is *&ldquo;clinical review was ordered last.&rdquo;*

This happens every time clinical review is positioned as a gate. The consultant becomes the bottleneck. The writer becomes the appellant. Nobody is happy. Velocity collapses. Quality degrades. Most importantly, the consultant — your most valuable editorial asset — develops a reputation in the marketing team for being difficult, when in fact they were correctly identifying that the structure of the piece was wrong.

## The reframe

Clinical review should be a *creative input,* not a checkpoint at the end. The consultant should be in the room when the brief is written, not when the draft is delivered.

This is not a clever process. It&rsquo;s the same process every good newsroom has used for a century — your editor doesn&rsquo;t read your piece for the first time on the day of publication. We just have to extend it across a domain wall: from the editorial team to the clinical team. The newsroom equivalent of *&ldquo;the consultant signs off at the end&rdquo;* would be *&ldquo;the editor reads the article only after it has been published.&rdquo;* No publication on earth does that.

## The five-step workflow

In practice, our workflow looks like this:

1. **Brief co-author with the consultant.** A 30-minute conversation. What is the patient question we&rsquo;re answering? What is the most common misunderstanding? What does the consultant wish every patient already knew? Two pages of notes come out of this meeting; they are the brief. The writer never starts a piece without them.

2. **Outline review with the consultant.** Before the writer touches the draft, the outline goes back to the consultant. *&ldquo;We&rsquo;re going to lead with X, then Y, then close with Z. Does the structure work?&rdquo;* This is where structural problems get caught — when they cost an email, not a rewrite. A 15-minute review at this stage saves three weeks of cycle time at the end.

3. **First draft to the editorial team.** The editor brings clarity, voice, structure. Not the consultant&rsquo;s job. The editorial team is shaping a *piece of writing* that happens to be about a clinical topic; the consultant is shaping a *piece of clinical thinking* that happens to be a piece of writing. Both jobs need doing; only one of them is the consultant&rsquo;s.

4. **Second draft to the consultant.** Now the consultant is reading a piece they had a hand in shaping. Corrections are line-level, not structural. The review takes 20 minutes instead of 90. The defensiveness disappears, because the consultant feels — accurately — that they&rsquo;ve been respected as a thinker, not used as a sign-off rubber-stamp.

5. **Final read-through together.** Optional, but valuable on the first three pieces with a new consultant. Builds the shared editorial muscle. After the third piece, this step usually drops away naturally — the consultant trusts the editorial process, and the editorial team has internalised the consultant&rsquo;s voice.

## What this changes

- **Velocity.** Articles ship in 3 weeks instead of 7.
- **Quality.** The clinical insight is in the structure, not bolted on at the end.
- **The consultant&rsquo;s relationship to the work.** They feel like an author, not a reviewer. They will say yes to the second piece. And the third. And by the tenth piece, they&rsquo;re proposing topics.
- **The editorial team&rsquo;s productivity.** No more rewrites for &ldquo;factual restructure.&rdquo; No more diplomatic emails managing a stalled review.
- **The patient.** The piece is more accurate, more useful, and more honest — because the clinical voice was upstream of the writing, not retrofitted to it.

## What it costs to set up

Almost nothing. The five-step workflow doesn&rsquo;t require a new tool, a new agency, or a new budget line. It requires a thirty-minute meeting at the start of every brief, and the willingness of the marketing team to treat the consultant as a co-author rather than a final reviewer.

The hospitals that resist this don&rsquo;t resist for capacity reasons. They resist because the marketing team is reluctant to ask consultants for upstream time, and the consultants — having only seen marketing as a downstream burden — are reluctant to give it. Both reluctances dissolve once the first piece ships in three weeks instead of seven.

## The shorter version

Clinical review is not a tax on creativity. It is creativity, when you put it in the right place.

Treat your senior consultants as editorial authors and they will give you a body of work the agency could never have produced. Treat them as gatekeepers and they will, correctly, become gates.

## FAQ

**Why does clinical review of hospital content always take so long?**
Because most hospitals position the consultant as the final gate, not as the upstream editor. By the time the consultant sees the draft, the structure has already been chosen, and structural changes require a rewrite. Move the consultant to the brief and outline stage and the review collapses from 90 minutes to 20.

**Who should sign off on hospital marketing content?**
Senior consultants in the relevant specialty for clinical accuracy, an in-house editor for voice and structure, and the marketing lead for brand and channel fit. Three sign-offs, in that order, with the consultant first — not last.

**How long should a hospital content piece take from brief to publication?**
Three weeks for an explainer, four for a procedure walkthrough, six for a patient story (which has consent and clinical-context overhead). Anything longer is a workflow problem, not a complexity problem.

**Should hospital marketing teams have full-time medical writers?**
Yes — at scale. Hospitals publishing more than two pieces per month benefit from a dedicated medical writer who has internalised the editorial voice and clinical conventions. Below that volume, freelancers with hospital experience are more cost-effective.

**What is the biggest mistake hospitals make in content production?**
Treating the consultant as a reviewer rather than a co-author. The consultant who is asked to *&ldquo;sign off&rdquo;* will block the piece; the consultant who is asked to *&ldquo;shape the brief&rdquo;* will champion it.
