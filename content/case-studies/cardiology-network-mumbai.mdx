## Where we started

Seventy videos. Two years of uploads. 600 monthly channel views. A senior cardiology team that had appeared on camera generously and watched their work disappear into the void.

The audit took 4 days. The diagnosis was familiar: every video had perfect production value, no thumbnail strategy, no playlist architecture, and no end-screen progression. The channel was operating like a TV station with no listings page.

## The first 8 weeks: nothing new

We didn&rsquo;t shoot a single video. We re-packaged.

- 70 thumbnails, redesigned in the consultant&rsquo;s likeness with a single visual question per frame.
- 70 titles, rewritten as the searches patients were already running.
- 14 playlists, organised by patient journey rather than by doctor.
- End-screen cards, every one of them, replaced with the next video in the journey playlist.

By week 6, monthly views had moved from 600 to 11,000. The algorithm noticed the watch-time lift first; the audience-growth signal followed.

## Weeks 9–24: shoot on strategy

With the existing content working, we identified the gaps. Twenty new videos, all shot in 4 production blocks, all mapped to specific journey stages and specific patient questions.

The most-watched single video — *&ldquo;What chest pain at night actually means&rdquo;* — was 7 minutes long and had been on the calendar for 18 months in concept. It now drives more enquiries per month than any single ad campaign the network has run.

## Outcomes at month 6

- **92,000 monthly views** (from 600).
- **4.8× the monthly enquiries** named *&ldquo;saw your YouTube&rdquo;* on the intake form.
- **34% of new patients** at the flagship hospital now report YouTube as a discovery channel — up from below the noise floor at month 0.
- **Cost per attributed enquiry: ₹47** (vs. ₹312 on Meta and ₹890 on Google Search at the same intent stage).

## What this changes about the network&rsquo;s strategy

The network is reallocating ~40% of its paid-search budget to YouTube content production for FY27. Not because YouTube is cheaper per click — it isn&rsquo;t — but because the assets compound. The page we wrote 18 months ago at the multispeciality hospital is still earning. The video we shot for this network in month 3 will still be earning in month 36.

That&rsquo;s the only argument that matters.
